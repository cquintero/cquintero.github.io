Learning Sabbatical

/ˈlərniNG səˈbadək(ə)l/
‍noun
  • Time away from work to develop new skills
  • A self-directed learning journey

What is a learning sabbatical?

Learning sabbaticals are time away from work focused on acquiring knowledge or skills. Most last between three months and two years.

‍

They are typically done full-time or with a minimal amount of part-time work. Some focus on research, projects, and short courses. Others include stints of contract or volunteer work. Most importantly, they are self-led.

Why?

Today's job market requires constant self-improvement. Yet, it's hard to focus on self-development while working a full-time job. Grad school, the default choice for a career break is increasingly unattractive. The price has risen out of control.

‍

‍

At the same time, many schools have grown out of touch with employer demands, failing to instill students with practical skills. The best way to learn is by doing. The best educators are now found online, not within any one school.

Why not just go to grad school?

Attending a masters or formal education program is still the best fit for most.

‍

They give attendees community and access to experts. They provide infrastructure for research and focused study. They impart credentials that signal intelligence, conscientiousness, and conformity —traits that employers value. Some fields, like medicine or law, are highly credential driven and don't lend themselves well to self-directed study.

‍

However, a learning sabbatical can be a great fit for those in fields where credentials matter less like entrepreneurship and software, or in creative fields like writing and design. Self-led learning allows for:

‍

  • High autonomy. We've all sat through required courses that were useless to our future goals.
  • Better content. We live in a golden age of educational material. Coursera. Youtube. Books on Kindle. The best educators are now found online, not within any one school.
  • Format flexibility. Unlike school where lectures dominate, learning sabbaticals allow you to choose the learning medium that makes most sense for the topic at hand, whether it's a project, book, short course, a series of interviews, or short-term work.
  • Subject matter recency. Especially for tech skills, degree programs lag job market demand. Learning sabbaticals give you focused time to get up to speed on the latest tools.
  • Low Cost. Learning sabbaticals cost a fraction of grad school.
  • Better Credential Signaling. In some fields, like software or design, a portfolio of projects carries more weight than academic pedigree.

‍
